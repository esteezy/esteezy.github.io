# blog title

_"Golden Handcuffs"_ has been a term that has lingered in my head for quite some time and to be frank, I'm not quite sure what kind of connotation it has. Typically, it relates to some incentive your job has that makes it much less feasible to leave than to stay. Could be a variety of things, but typically relates to some aspect of one's total compensation (i.e. RSUs, bonuses, IPO, etc). For example, suppose you had a deferred bonus for $100k paid out in 25% installments over the next 4 years. You're bored with your job and want to move on, you're manager is satisfied with your performance, company outlook looks good, and your next vesting is in a few months. So what do you do? Why not just wait it out, then move on? So you do and get that payout, you start looking at jobs and realize that there have been mass layoffs. Yikes. Maybe should wait it out while the industry recovers. A few months go by, you've seen a few positions open up that seems interesting but the paycut is just really not aligned with what you're making now. Well, funny enough your next vesting schedule is coming up! Might as well wait. Whether or not that scenario is good or bad is an opinion you'll have to make. 

### It can't all be about money, right?
Well, no it doesn't have to be and it isn't. Right now I work in the government as a systems software engineer. The __number one__ thing that always gets brought up when mentioning about benefits is stability. They are borderline synonymous. You won't get fired....ever (well, assuming you aren't a baboon in the workplace). Economy goes into a recession? You might not get paid this week, but you still have a job (yes, you'll still need to work btw)! Mass layoffs of engineers? Not here! Restructuring? We welcome it with open arms!

It certainly is a benefit that many people value. However, this leads to a specific makeup of engineers. Following closely is health benefits. Now at some point I would like to actually sit down and see how that benefit stacks up against private sector plans, but the basic guideline is that the government subsidizes approximately 70% of your premium, which is nice at face value. Both of those benefits are strong indicators for "Golden Handcuffs", but it comes with an interesting caveat that differs than its financial counterparts.
